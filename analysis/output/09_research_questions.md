# Research Questions

**RQ1 — Which persuasion strategy changes model beliefs most frequently?** In this sample, Majority vs credibility had the highest flip rate (19.5%, 95% CI [14.6, 25.5]%), compared with Strength vs credibility (13.5%). See flip-rate omnibus test in statistical tests.

**RQ2 — When persuasion strategies conflict, which strategy is selected most often?** In this sample, **strength** had the highest pooled win rate (63.3%, 95% CI [59.4, 67.1]% across all competitive trials). A chi-square test against a uniform three-way split gave χ² = 303.84, *p* = 1.052e-66 (pooled *n* = 600); this does not establish pairwise dominance in any single matchup.

**RQ3 — Does persuasion improve or reduce factual accuracy?** In this sample, mean accuracy change from prior across persuasion conditions was +9.21 percentage points; mean net persuasion gain was +9.21 percentage points (helpful − harmful). Condition-level significance is reported via Holm-corrected McNemar tests.

**RQ4 — Does persuasion mainly change beliefs or simply reduce confidence?** In this sample, mean flip rate was 16.2% while mean confidence change was -1.05 points, suggesting persuasion primarily shifted answers in this benchmark.

**RQ5 — Are some models more resistant to persuasion than others?** In this sample, the lowest flip rate was qwen/qwen3.5-122b-a10b (3.4%) and the highest was meta/llama-3.1-8b-instruct (29.0%). See model-comparison tests for omnibus significance.

**RQ6 — Does credibility outperform majority?** In this sample, credibility-alone flip rate was 16.0% vs majority-plain 17.0% and majority-vague 19.0%. In majority-vs-credibility matchups, majority led (93.5%, 95% CI [89.2, 96.2]%; binomial *p* = 1.18e-40).

**RQ7 — Does strong evidence consistently outperform majority?** In this sample, strength-alone flip rate was 14.0% vs majority-plain 17.0% and majority-vague 19.0%. In strength-vs-majority matchups, strength led (99.5%, 95% CI [97.2, 99.9]%; binomial *p* = 2.502e-58).
